Correlation-Based Arbitrage Strategy for ASX Equities

Comprehensive Research Report — Prepared for Atlas-ASX Project — 17 February 2026

Executive Summary

This report synthesizes findings from 15+ academic papers (2022-2025), practitioner frameworks, and specialized library documentation to provide actionable guidance for implementing pairs trading on ASX equities.

Key Findings:

  • Pairs trading remains profitable (6.2% annual excess returns, Sharpe 1.35) as of 2024, contrary to claims of strategy decay (Zhu, Yale 2024)
  • The distance method provides the highest raw returns (91 bps/month), while cointegration offers marginally better risk-adjusted performance and superior crisis-period behavior
  • Optimal z-score entry threshold is 1.42 (not the commonly cited 2.0), with exit at 0.37
  • Standard z-score approaches outperform Ornstein-Uhlenbeck models in practice (Sharpe 0.779 vs 0.468)
  • Transaction costs reduce returns by 50-90% — the single most critical factor for ASX implementation
  • Critical constraint for Atlas-ASX: $5,000 AUD equity base is insufficient for meaningful pairs trading. Minimum viable capital is approximately $25,000-50,000 AUD for 5 simultaneously active pairs

Primary Recommendation: Defer full pairs trading implementation until equity base reaches $25,000+ AUD. In the interim, enhance existing mean-reversion strategy with cointegration-based pair signals as a signal filter rather than a separate strategy.

A.2 Correlation vs Cointegration: The Definitive Comparison

Correlation (Pearson/Spearman)

  • What it measures: Linear co-movement of returns/prices over a window
  • Strengths: Fast to compute, intuitive, excellent as a pre-filter
  • Critical weakness: Does NOT guarantee mean-reversion of the spread. Two stocks can be highly correlated yet their spread can drift (non-stationary). Correlation is necessary but not sufficient for pairs trading.
  • Best use: Pre-filter to reduce pair universe before cointegration testing. Threshold: Spearman ρ ≥ 0.6 minimum, ≥ 0.75 preferred.

Cointegration (Engle-Granger Two-Step)

  • What it measures: Whether a linear combination of two price series is stationary (mean-reverting)
  • Step 1: Regress Y = β · X + ε using OLS to get hedge ratio β
  • Step 2: Test residuals for stationarity using ADF test (p-value < 0.05)
  • Strengths: Directly tests mean-reversion. Provides hedge ratio. Theoretically sound.
  • Weaknesses: Asymmetric (result depends on which stock is Y vs X). Assumes constant hedge ratio. N(N-1)/2 tests required.

Cointegration (Johansen Test)

  • Advantage over EG: Symmetric (no dependent/independent variable choice needed). Can test multiple assets simultaneously.
  • Disadvantage: More complex. Computationally heavier. Sensitive to lag selection.
  • Best use: Multi-asset basket construction (3+ assets) or when you need symmetry.
Verdict: Cointegration is Superior to Correlation for Pair Validation
  • Use correlation (Spearman ρ ≥ 0.6) as a fast pre-filter to reduce the search space
  • Apply Engle-Granger cointegration test (p < 0.05) as the primary validation
  • Huck & Afawubo (2015): cointegration yields higher profit than distance when pairs are pre-screened
  • Cointegration excels in crisis periods: Sortino ratio rises from 0.63 to 0.84 (Rad et al.)
  • Use Johansen only for multi-asset basket construction

A.3 Distance-Based Methods

The classical Gatev et al. (2006) approach:

  1. Normalize all price series to start at $1 during the formation period
  2. Calculate Sum of Squared Differences (SSD) between all pairs of normalized prices
  3. Select the top-N pairs with the smallest SSD
  4. During the trading period, open positions when spread exceeds 2 standard deviations
Key Finding (Zhu, Yale 2024): Using the top 500 closest pairs instead of top 20 achieves the highest Sharpe ratio (1.35 vs ~0.8) because diversification averages out outliers from delistings and stock splits. This is counter-intuitive but empirically robust over 2003-2023. The "wait-one-day" rule (open position one day after divergence signal) is critical to avoid bid-ask bounce.

A.5 Sector-Based Pre-Filtering

  • Advantage: Reduces computational cost. Pairs have economic rationale.
  • Disadvantage: Most profitable pairs are already well-known and heavily traded, reducing alpha.
  • Evidence: Zhu (2024) tested industry-restricted pairs [R20] — only marginally different from unrestricted [T20] (Sharpe ~0.8), but significantly worse than diversified [T500] (Sharpe 1.35).
Warning for ASX: The ASX is heavily concentrated in Financials and Materials. Sector pre-filtering would overweight these sectors. For a 159-ticker universe, the full 12,561 pair scan is computationally feasible without sector filtering.

A.6 Recommended Pair Selection Pipeline for Atlas-ASX

Recommended Multi-Stage Pipeline:
  1. Stage 1 — Correlation Pre-Filter: Rolling 252-day Spearman correlation for all 12,561 pairs. Keep ρ ≥ 0.65. (~30 seconds)
  2. Stage 2 — Cointegration Test: Engle-Granger on surviving pairs (~1,000-2,000). Keep p < 0.05. (~2-5 minutes)
  3. Stage 3 — Mean-Reversion Quality: Half-life 5-60 days, Hurst < 0.45, ≥1 crossing/month
  4. Stage 4 — Liquidity Filter: Both stocks must have median daily value traded > $500K AUD
  5. Stage 5 — Ranking: Composite score of cointegration p-value (lower = better), half-life (shorter preferred), spread volatility (moderate preferred)
  6. Stage 6 — Portfolio Construction: Select top 5-20 pairs with sector diversification constraint

B. Signal Generation

B.1 Z-Score of Spread

The z-score is the most widely used and empirically robust signal for pairs trading. It measures how many standard deviations the current spread deviates from its rolling mean.

Z-Score Formula:
spread = price_Y - β · price_X
z_score = (spread - rolling_mean(spread, window)) / rolling_std(spread, window)

Standard Rules: Enter when |z| > threshold, exit when z crosses zero or opposite threshold.

Critical Finding: The optimal entry threshold is 1.42, NOT the traditional 2.0. Zhu (2024) showed that lower thresholds capture more convergence opportunities while maintaining positive expected returns. The entry at 2.0 misses ~40% of profitable trades. Exit threshold of 0.37 (partial convergence) outperforms waiting for full convergence (0.0), because the last portion of convergence is slowest and carries regime-change risk.
Recommended Z-Score Parameters for Atlas-ASX:
  • Rolling window: 60-90 trading days (shorter windows adapt faster but are noisier)
  • Entry threshold: 1.5 (slightly more conservative than 1.42 due to ASX spread costs)
  • Exit threshold: 0.5 (slightly wider than 0.37 for same reason)
  • Stop-loss: |z| > 4.0 (pair breakdown assumed)
  • Maximum holding period: 60 trading days (time-based stop)

B.2 Kalman Filter for Dynamic Hedge Ratios

The static OLS hedge ratio from cointegration testing assumes a constant relationship between pairs. In practice, the relationship drifts over time. The Kalman filter provides an online, adaptive hedge ratio that tracks the time-varying relationship.

How It Works

  1. State equation: The hedge ratio β(t) follows a random walk: β(t) = β(t-1) + w(t), where w ~ N(0, Q)
  2. Observation equation: price_Y(t) = β(t) · price_X(t) + v(t), where v ~ N(0, R)
  3. The Kalman filter recursion updates β(t) at each timestep, providing:
    • Estimated hedge ratio (state mean)
    • Uncertainty of the estimate (state covariance)
    • Forecast error (innovation) which IS the spread signal

Kalman Filter vs Static OLS

FeatureStatic OLSKalman Filter
Hedge ratioFixed over formation periodUpdates every bar
AdaptationNone until re-estimationContinuous
Spread stationarityDegrades over timeBetter maintained
ImplementationSimple OLS regressionModerate (state-space model)
Overfitting riskLowerHigher (Q, R tuning)
Best forStable long-term pairsDrifting relationships
Kalman Filter Parameters:
  • Transition covariance (Q): Start with small value (1e-5). Controls how fast the hedge ratio can change. Too large = noisy, too small = slow adaptation.
  • Observation covariance (R): Estimate from initial residual variance. Controls how much weight is given to new observations vs prior estimate.
  • Initial state: Set to OLS beta from formation period
  • Initial covariance: Set to 1.0 (moderate uncertainty)
  • Spread = forecast error (innovation) from the Kalman filter, already z-score-like
Practical Warning: Kalman filter adds complexity without guaranteed improvement. The arxiv 2024 paper showed standard z-score outperforming OU-model approaches (Sharpe 0.779 vs 0.468). Use Kalman filter only when you have evidence that static hedge ratios are degrading for specific pairs. For initial implementation, static OLS with periodic re-estimation (every 60-90 days) is recommended.

B.3 Ornstein-Uhlenbeck Process Parameters

The OU process models the spread as a continuous-time mean-reverting stochastic process:

dS(t) = λ(μ - S(t))dt + σdW(t)

Parameter Estimation (from AR(1) regression)

ParameterMeaningEstimationTypical Range
λ (kappa)Mean-reversion speedλ = -ln(a) / δt, where a is AR(1) coefficient0.01 - 5.0 (higher = faster reversion)
μLong-term meanμ = b / (1 - a), where b is AR(1) interceptVaries by pair
σVolatilityσ = sd(ε) · sqrt(-2ln(a) / (δt(1-a²)))Varies by pair

OU-Based Optimal Trading Thresholds (HudsonThames)

The optimal stopping problem for OU processes yields analytical solutions for entry/exit thresholds that maximize expected profit per unit time. The arbitragelab library implements this directly.

Empirical Evidence Against OU: Despite theoretical elegance, OU-based strategies consistently underperform simple z-score approaches in backtests:
  • Arxiv 2024: Standard model Sharpe 0.779 vs OU model 0.468
  • Standard model max drawdown -5.91% vs OU -8.99%
  • Authors attribute this to suboptimal parameter tuning and non-stationarity of some pairs
Recommendation: Use OU parameters (especially λ) as a filter for pair quality, not as a direct signal generator. High λ pairs have faster mean-reversion and are better candidates.

B.4 Half-Life of Mean Reversion

Half-life measures how long, on average, the spread takes to revert halfway to its mean. It is the single most important filter for pair quality.

Half-Life Calculation:
1. Run AR(1) regression on spread: S(t) = a · S(t-1) + b + ε
2. Half-life = -ln(2) / ln(a)

Alternatively from OU parameters: Half-life = ln(2) / λ

Half-Life Filter Guidelines

Half-Life RangeInterpretationTrading Suitability

1-5 daysVery fast reversionMarginal: may be bid-ask bounce. High turnover costs.
5-30 daysOptimal sweet spotExcellent: fast enough to profit, slow enough to execute
30-60 daysModerate reversionGood: lower turnover, larger moves needed

> 120 daysVery slow / non-revertingNot suitable for pairs trading
Recommended Half-Life Filter for Atlas-ASX:
  • Minimum: 5 days (avoid microstructure noise)
  • Maximum: 60 days (avoid capital lock-up and regime risk)
  • Sweet spot: 10-30 days (balances frequency and profitability)
  • Use in conjunction with Hurst exponent < 0.5 for confirmation
  • Minimum 1 mean-crossing per month (empirical mean-reversion confirmation)

B.5 Comprehensive Signal Pipeline

Recommended Signal Generation Architecture:
  1. Calculate Spread: spread = price_Y - β · price_X (using OLS β from formation period)
  2. Calculate Z-Score: z = (spread - rolling_mean_60d) / rolling_std_60d
  3. Entry Signal: |z| > 1.5 (long spread if z < -1.5, short spread if z > 1.5)
  4. Exit Signal: |z| < 0.5 (partial convergence) OR holding period > 60 days
  5. Stop-Loss: |z| > 4.0 (pair breakdown)
  6. Re-estimate β: Every 60-90 trading days using rolling OLS
  7. Re-validate pair: Monthly cointegration re-test; drop pair if p > 0.10

C. Risk Management

C.1 Stop-Loss Approaches for Pairs Trades

Approach 1: Z-Score Based Stop-Loss (Recommended)

  • Close position if |z-score| exceeds maximum divergence threshold (typically 4.0-5.0)
  • Rationale: If spread widens to 4+ standard deviations, the pair relationship has likely broken down
  • Advantage: Directly tied to the signal mechanism, easy to implement
  • Disadvantage: During regime changes, the z-score denominator (rolling std) may be stale

Approach 2: Individualized Quantile-Based Stop-Loss

  • From the EconStor copula pairs trading paper: set stop-loss at the 10th percentile of cumulative return distribution observed during the formation period
  • Each pair gets its own tailored stop-loss level based on historical behavior
  • Performance: Sharpe ratios of 1.52 (mean-reversion) and 1.33 (momentum) with these stops
  • More sophisticated but requires historical simulation for each pair

Approach 3: Time-Based Stop

  • Close any position held longer than maximum holding period (60-90 trading days)
  • Prevents capital lock-up in non-converging trades
  • Atlas-ASX finding: Time exits have 90% win rate, suggesting this is highly effective

Approach 4: Dollar-Based Stop-Loss

  • Set maximum loss per pair trade as a fixed dollar amount or percentage of equity (e.g., 1-2%)
  • Simple, consistent, but not tied to spread dynamics
Recommended Stop-Loss Configuration:
  • Primary: Z-score based, |z| > 4.0 triggers immediate close
  • Secondary: Time-based, 60 trading day maximum holding period
  • Tertiary: Dollar-based, max 2% equity loss per pair position
  • All three stops should be active simultaneously; whichever triggers first closes the position

C.3 Position Sizing for Dollar-Neutral Pairs

Dollar-Neutral Construction

A dollar-neutral pair position means the dollar value of the long leg equals the dollar value of the short leg:

  • If long $1,000 of Stock A, short $1,000 of Stock B
  • Net market exposure = $0 (theoretically market-neutral)
  • Actual P&L depends on spread movement, not market direction

Position Sizing Methods

MethodFormulaProsCons
Equal Dollar$V long = $V short = equity / (2 * N_pairs)Simple, balancedIgnores volatility differences
Volatility-WeightedWeight inversely by volatility of each legBetter risk balanceMore complex, needs vol estimates
Beta-NeutralWeight by hedge ratio βMinimizes systematic riskBeta must be estimated accurately
Position Sizing Example for Atlas-ASX ($5,000 equity, 5 pairs):
  • Capital per pair: $5,000 / 5 = $1,000
  • Each leg: $500 long + $500 short = $1,000 total capital deployed
  • But: $500 positions on ASX face significant spread costs (0.08% commission + ~0.2-0.5% spread on mid-caps)
  • Minimum viable position: ~$2,000 per leg to keep costs under 0.5% round-trip
  • This implies a minimum of $20,000 for 5 pairs (2 * $2,000 * 5)
Critical Capital Constraint for Atlas-ASX: With $5,000 equity, even 2 simultaneous pairs would require $2,500 per pair ($1,250 per leg). At ASX mid-cap spreads of 0.3-0.5%, the round-trip cost per leg is ~$4-6, totaling $8-12 per pair trade. On a $2,500 position, that is 0.3-0.5% just in costs, before commissions of 0.08% each way. Expected pairs trading returns of 30-90 bps/month are almost entirely consumed by these costs at small position sizes. Pairs trading is not viable at $5,000 equity on ASX.

C.4 Handling Pair Breakdowns (Regime Changes)

Detection Methods

  1. Rolling Cointegration Test: Re-run Engle-Granger monthly. If p-value rises above 0.10, flag pair as deteriorating. Above 0.20, remove from portfolio.
  2. CUSUM Test: Cumulative sum of forecast errors detects structural breaks in the spread relationship
  3. Regime Filter: Track rolling correlation. If it drops below 0.4, pair is likely in breakdown
  4. Spread Variance Monitoring: If rolling variance of spread doubles vs formation period, relationship is unstable

Response Protocol

SignalAction
p-value rises above 0.10Reduce position size by 50%, tighten stop-loss to |z| > 3.0
p-value rises above 0.20Close all positions in this pair, remove from portfolio
Rolling correlation drops below 0.4Close all positions, quarantine pair for 60 days
Corporate action (merger, delisting)Immediate close at market
Sector-wide shockReview all pairs in affected sector, tighten stops
Key Insight from Literature: The EconStor paper handles pair breakdowns through: (1) minimum Spearman ρ ≥ 0.6 during pair selection, (2) individualized stop-loss from 10th percentile of returns, (3) automatic close at end of 12-month trading period, (4) no pyramiding (max 1 position per pair), and (5) re-estimation of dependence structure every 12 months. This multi-layered approach achieves Sharpe ratios > 1.3.

E.4 Minimal Implementation Code Skeleton

import numpy as np import pandas as pd from statsmodels.tsa.stattools import coint, adfuller from sklearn.cluster import OPTICS from sklearn.decomposition import PCA def find_cointegrated_pairs(prices_df, significance=0.05): """Find all cointegrated pairs from a DataFrame of prices.""" n = prices_df.shape[1] tickers = prices_df.columns pairs = [] for i in range(n): for j in range(i+1, n): # Pre-filter: correlation check corr = prices_df.iloc[:, i].corr(prices_df.iloc[:, j]) if corr < 0.65: continue # Cointegration test score, pvalue, _ = coint(prices_df.iloc[:, i], prices_df.iloc[:, j]) if pvalue < significance: pairs.append((tickers[i], tickers[j], pvalue, corr)) return sorted(pairs, key=lambda x: x[2]) # Sort by p-value def calculate_half_life(spread): """Calculate half-life of mean reversion from spread series.""" spread_lag = spread.shift(1).dropna() spread_ret = spread.diff().dropna() spread_lag = spread_lag.iloc[1:] beta = np.polyfit(spread_lag, spread_ret, 1)[0] half_life = -np.log(2) / np.log(1 + beta) return half_life def calculate_zscore(spread, window=60): """Calculate rolling z-score of spread.""" mean = spread.rolling(window).mean() std = spread.rolling(window).std() return (spread - mean) / std

F.2 Modern ML Approaches vs Classical Methods

Deep Learning for Pairs Trading

  • LSTM/GRU networks for spread prediction: Promising in-sample, but severe overfitting out-of-sample. Transaction costs typically eliminate any advantage over z-score.
  • Reinforcement Learning (RL) for optimal entry/exit: Theoretically elegant but requires massive data (millions of episodes). Not practical for ASX with limited history.
  • Transformer models: Emerging research area. No robust out-of-sample evidence yet for pairs trading specifically.

ML Clustering for Pair Selection

  • OPTICS/DBSCAN with PCA (Sarmento & Horta): Most promising ML application. Finds non-obvious pairs that traditional methods miss. Reduces volatility of pairs portfolio.
  • Autoencoders for feature extraction: Replace PCA with non-linear feature learning. Marginal improvement over PCA in limited studies.
  • Graph Neural Networks: Model stock relationships as a graph. Cutting-edge but not yet validated for pairs trading.
ML Reality Check: No ML approach has consistently demonstrated out-of-sample superiority over classical cointegration + z-score in the pairs trading domain. The primary value of ML is in pair selection (clustering) and feature engineering, not in signal generation. For Atlas-ASX with its small universe (159 tickers) and limited capital, classical methods are strongly preferred.

F.3 Copula-Based Approaches

How Copulas Work for Pairs Trading

  1. Transform each stock’s returns into uniform marginals using empirical CDF or parametric distribution
  2. Fit a copula function (t-copula, Clayton, Gumbel, Frank) to capture the dependence structure
  3. Generate conditional probability signals: “given stock A returned X, the probability of stock B returning less than Y is Z%”
  4. Trade when conditional probabilities indicate extreme mispricing

H.5 Risk Considerations

Risks Specific to ASX Pairs Trading:
  • Sector Concentration: ASX is dominated by Financials (CBA, WBC, NAB, ANZ) and Materials (BHP, RIO, FMG). Most obvious pairs are in these sectors, creating sector concentration risk.
